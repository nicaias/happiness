The psychology and neuroscience of ruminative behavior



The Psychology


Rumination is defined as repeatedly and persistently thinking negatively about ourselves, our feelings, personal concerns, and upsetting experiences. If repeated over time, it can become a key contributor to major depressive disorder (MDD).


When we fall into rumination, our self-focus intensifies, which reinforces a cycle of negative mood and negative thinking. We start comparing our current situation to where we think we should be, focusing on the differences rather than the similarities. As this continues, it creates a downward mental spiral where rumination heightens negative emotion, increases uncertainty, lowers confidence in our plans, and leads to avoidance or other dysregulated behaviors as we try to escape the discomfort it creates.

Prolonged rumination and worry have been shown to predict increased heart disease over a 20-year follow-up prospectively, and levels of rumination are significantly higher among individuals with chronic health conditions.

In adolescents, rumination predicted increased emotional abuse and victimization from peers at 8 and 8.4 months follow-up.

In patients with depression, rumination predicted relationship difficulties and increased submissive interpersonal styles.

Rumination elevates cortisol and prolongs duration of cortisol activation in young women, female high-trait ruminators, undergradate students completing difficult anagram tasks, and patients with depression.


Through continuous acts of rumination, long-term well-being and health become much harder to maintain. Our physical health is the foundation of almost everything we do, making it crucial that we take care of our mind, so that our body does not suffer the consequences.



The Neuroscience


The Default Mode Network


The default mode network, or the DMN, becomes active when our brains begin to wander into daydreaming, reminiscing, or self-reflection.

Two cortexes, the ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) and the posterior cingulate cortex (PCC), make up the DMN in the brain. They work to both assign value to various stimuli, and relate our situations back to ourselves in a spatiotemporal way.


Rumination in The Brain


While self-reflection is a natural process for us to analyze our past choices and adapt accordingly, rumination happens when this process becomes distorted. This is caused by an increased connectivity between the DMN and the subgenual prefrontal cortex (sgPFC) in the medial-dorsal thalamus (MDT).

In a study with depressed individuals, they could be characterized by this increased connectivity which was then able to predict the levels of rumination in MDD.
